A Doctor's Life

Circa 2020

Photojournalism project done during a program with the New York Times Summer Journalism program. Curated during the height of the pandemic, this photo-journalism piece centers on one doctor (Cindy Klipfel) and her experience with the daily struggles of being in children's healthcare during the virus' beginning.

August 2020 - East Greenwich Pediatrics

Before COVID-19, East Greenwich Pediatrics had a thriving community of families who moved through their busy doors each day. They took time and care with each one of them, ensuring that the children received the best care possible. Early on in January the staff, most notably Dr. McMillan, sensed an urgency from the news of the spread of the virus. They ordered N95 masks, gowns, and extra supplies in case it came to the United States. When it did, they were prepared. For months they had a rotating schedule, with two ‘pods’ of doctors and nurses per week, one who would be at home conducting tele-health visits, and the other who would see patients. Now they have finally switched to a more regular schedule, but life in the office is still greatly impacted by the pandemic. Patients at first were less willing to come to the office, but doctors worked out tele-health visits to keep both patient and doctor safe. Tele-health is an online platform much like face-time or zoom that creates a face-to-face connection with doctor and patient. Its widespread use has been enormously helpful in continuing care during these unprecedented times, although the occasional glitch in connection can make it hard.

The help of technology is immense, as online records also create a platform where work can be done from home. However now that patients feel more comfortable coming into the office, their day is fully scheduled. Yearly well-visits and vaccinations that had been postponed, are now trying to be reworked into the schedule. Dr. Klipfel is one of the four hardworking doctors at East Greenwich Pediatrics. She works four days a week and one weekend a month, with ten hour shifts in the office each day. On this day, she tested 12 patients in the tent in the morning, and saw 9 patients in the office in the afternoon. In between, she has a 30 minute break for lunch during which she makes up for phone calls that have been piling up since 8am. The work is hard, but the outcome is rewarding, as Rhode Island has been one of the most ‘corona safe’ states in all of the United States. There is a lot of work to do, but East Greenwich Pediatrics is doing their share of creating a safe place for their community.



In the 'gown room,' N95 masks are hung on the walls, as if a part of the artwork. In this room the staff don their protective gear, staying safe from COVID-19. The masks are hung, as the staff and doctors are forced to use a mask for the whole week, rather than switching between patients as should be standard in this situation. The mask shortages make it hard to rotate as frequently.

Dr. Klipfel ties her gown, making her outfit almost complete. The only thing left is gloves; today she had to go up a size as the heat was making it too hard to put them on.

Small animal depictions, a warm welcome to the small children who come here.

Danielle and Jamie, medical assistants, put up the third wall of the testing tent to keep in the AC. The day recorded temperatures over 100 degrees, and the flimsy air conditioner doesn't do much.

Jamie, an assistant who started working in December, barely before the pandemic hit, sets up the stations to set down the face shields between patients. She labels them with sharpie, making sure to distinguish between dirty and clean.

As the first two patients did not show up for their appointments, Dr. Klipfel talks with her coworkers to figure out a way to make the system more cohesive.

Dr. Klipfel examines a patient, who sits on her mother’s lap as she prepares to get tested for COVID-19. After getting tested, they will quarantine for 2-3 days before getting results back.

Dr. Klipfel wears sleek shoes comfortable for standing around all day, contrasting the well-worn sneakers and Velcro slip-on sneakers of her patients.

A child walks through the halls, unaware of why the doctors wear masks. Too young to comprehend, she will grow up in the world post-pandemic.

In between patients, Dr. Klipfel tries to write down notes on her computer, reminding herself of each patient and what happened in those few minutes. Her office, the ground zero for her short-lived breaks, is scattered with various cleaning supplies, leaving her always prepared for the next patient.

Dr. Klipfel takes a deep breath.

After taking off her gear for a quick sip of her water, Dr. Klipfel puts her mask back on.

Dr. Klipfel switches gloves and gown between patients, new precautions set up that make sure not to contaminate germs from one patient to the next. In this photo, she stands facing the door out to the testing tent, where she performs tests every fifteen minutes from 8:00 am to 1:00 pm.

The evening light comes through the window of an empty exam room. After 10 hours, Dr. Klipfel is finally going home.
